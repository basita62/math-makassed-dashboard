1. Finding GCD using Divisors Listings

Focus: Listing all divisors to find the Greatest Common Divisor for 2 or 3 numbers.

10 Solved Examples

1. GCD(12, 18): $D_{12} = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12\}$, $D_{18} = \{1, 2, 3, 6, 9, 18\}$. Common: $\{1, 2, 3, 6\}$. GCD = 6
2. GCD(8, 20): $D_{8} = \{1, 2, 4, 8\}$, $D_{20} = \{1, 2, 4, 5, 10, 20\}$. Common: $\{1, 2, 4\}$. GCD = 4
3. GCD(30, 45): $D_{30} = \{1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 10, 15, 30\}$, $D_{45} = \{1, 3, 5, 9, 15, 45\}$. Common: $\{1, 3, 5, 15\}$. GCD = 15
4. GCD(7, 11): $D_{7} = \{1, 7\}$, $D_{11} = \{1, 11\}$. Common: $\{1\}$. GCD = 1
5. GCD(24, 36): $D_{24} = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 8, 12, 24\}$, $D_{36} = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36\}$. Common: $\{1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12\}$. GCD = 12
6. GCD(10, 100): $D_{10} = \{1, 2, 5, 10\}$, $D_{100} = \{1, 2, 4, 5, 10, 20, ...\}$. Common: $\{1, 2, 5, 10\}$. GCD = 10
7. GCD(6, 8, 10): $D_{6} = \{1, 2, 3, 6\}$, $D_{8} = \{1, 2, 4, 8\}$, $D_{10} = \{1, 2, 5, 10\}$. Common: $\{1, 2\}$. GCD = 2
8. GCD(15, 25, 40): $D_{15} = \{1, 3, 5, 15\}$, $D_{25} = \{1, 5, 25\}$, $D_{40} = \{1, 2, 4, 5, 8, 10, 20, 40\}$. Common: $\{1, 5\}$. GCD = 5
9. GCD(4, 16, 64): $D_{4} = \{1, 2, 4\}$, $D_{16} = \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16\}$, $D_{64} = \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64\}$. Common: $\{1, 2, 4\}$. GCD = 4
10. GCD(1, 50): $D_{1} = \{1\}$, $D_{50} = \{1, 2, ...\}$. Common: $\{1\}$. GCD = 1

3. Checking if a Number is Prime or Composite

Focus: Divide by consecutive primes. Stop when the quotient is less than the divisor. If no prime up to that point divides the number, it is prime.

10 Solved Examples

1. Is 29 prime? Primes: 2 (No), 3 (No), 5 (No). $29 \div 7 = 4$ rem 1. Since $4 < 7$, stop. Result: Prime
2. Is 91 prime? Primes: 2 (No), 3 (No), 5 (No), 7 (Yes: $91 \div 7 = 13$). Result: Composite ($7 \times 13$)
3. Is 83 prime? Primes: 2, 3, 5, 7 (No). $83 \div 11 = 7$ rem 6. Since $7 < 11$, stop. Result: Prime
4. Is 51 prime? Primes: 2 (No), 3 (Yes: $51 \div 3 = 17$). Result: Composite ($3 \times 17$)
5. Is 121 prime? Primes: 2, 3, 5, 7 (No), 11 (Yes: $121 \div 11 = 11$). Result: Composite ($11 \times 11$)
6. Is 13 prime? Primes: 2 (No), 3 (No). $13 \div 5 = 2$ rem 3. Since $2 < 5$, stop. Result: Prime
7. Is 143 prime? Primes: 2, 3, 5, 7 (No), 11 (Yes: $143 \div 11 = 13$). Result: Composite ($11 \times 13$)
8. Is 57 prime? Primes: 2 (No), 3 (Yes: $57 \div 3 = 19$). Result: Composite ($3 \times 19$)
9. Is 101 prime? Primes: 2, 3, 5, 7 (No). $101 \div 11 = 9$ rem 2. Since $9 < 11$, stop. Result: Prime
10. Is 49 prime? Primes: 2, 3, 5 (No), 7 (Yes: $49 \div 7 = 7$). Result: Composite ($7 \times 7$)

4. Finding GCD and LCM using Prime Factorization

Focus: Using lowest powers for GCD and highest powers for LCM, with numbers up to 500.

10 Solved Examples

1. GCD/LCM(12, 18): $12 = 2^2 \times 3$, $18 = 2 \times 3^2$. GCD = $2 \times 3 = 6$. LCM = $2^2 \times 3^2 = 36$.
2. GCD/LCM(48, 72): $48 = 2^4 \times 3$, $72 = 2^3 \times 3^2$. GCD = $2^3 \times 3 = 24$. LCM = $2^4 \times 3^2 = 144$.
3. GCD/LCM(90, 100): $90 = 2 \times 3^2 \times 5$, $100 = 2^2 \times 5^2$. GCD = $2 \times 5 = 10$. LCM = $2^2 \times 3^2 \times 5^2 = 900$.
4. GCD/LCM(30, 60, 84): $30=2{\times}3{\times}5$, $60=2^2{\times}3{\times}5$, $84=2^2{\times}3{\times}7$. GCD = $2{\times}3=6$. LCM = $2^2{\times}3{\times}5{\times}7=420$.
5. GCD/LCM(105, 441): $105 = 3 \times 5 \times 7$, $441 = 3^2 \times 7^2$. GCD = $3 \times 7 = 21$. LCM = $3^2 \times 5 \times 7^2 = 2205$.
6. GCD/LCM(140, 500): $140 = 2^2 \times 5 \times 7$, $500 = 2^2 \times 5^3$. GCD = $2^2 \times 5 = 20$. LCM = $2^2 \times 5^3 \times 7 = 3500$.
7. GCD/LCM(6, 8, 10): $6=2{\times}3$, $8=2^3$, $10=2{\times}5$. GCD = $2$. LCM = $2^3{\times}3{\times}5 = 120$.
8. GCD/LCM(210, 315): $210 = 2 \times 3 \times 5 \times 7$, $315 = 3^2 \times 5 \times 7$. GCD = $3 \times 5 \times 7 = 105$. LCM = $2 \times 3^2 \times 5 \times 7 = 630$.
9. GCD/LCM(9, 27): $9 = 3^2$, $27 = 3^3$. GCD = $3^2 = 9$. LCM = $3^3 = 27$.
10. GCD/LCM(32, 256): $32 = 2^5$, $256 = 2^8$. GCD = $2^5 = 32$. LCM = $2^8 = 256$.

5. Word Problems: GCD vs. LCM

When to use GCD (Greatest Common Divisor):

Look for keywords like: Split, divide, largest group, greatest amount, shelf arrangement, maximum size.

When to use LCM (Least Common Multiple):

Look for keywords like: Repeat, occur together again, simultaneous, smallest distance, cycles.

10 Solved Examples

1. Lighthouses: One light flashes every 20s, another every 42s. When will they flash together again?

Concept: LCM(20, 42). $20=2^2 \times 5$, $42=2 \times 3 \times 7$. LCM = $2^2 \times 3 \times 5 \times 7 = 420$s.

2. Shelving Books: 36 Math and 48 Science books must be stacked in equal piles. Largest pile size?

Concept: GCD(36, 48) = 12 books per pile.

3. Race Track: Runner A takes 12 min, Runner B takes 18 min. When do they meet at the start?

Concept: LCM(12, 18) = 36 minutes.

4. Fruit Baskets: 30 apples and 45 oranges divided into identical baskets. Max baskets possible?

Concept: GCD(30, 45) = 15 baskets.

5. School Bells: Bells ring every 15, 20, and 30 min. If they ring at 8:00 AM, when is the next time?

Concept: LCM(15, 20, 30) = 60 min. Result: 9:00 AM.

6. Fabric Cutting: Two rolls (80cm and 100cm) cut into equal strips. What is the max strip length?

Concept: GCD(80, 100) = 20 cm.

7. Grouping Students: Groups of 6, 8, or 10 all leave 0 students left over. Smallest class size?

Concept: LCM(6, 8, 10) = 120 students.

8. String Lengths: Pieces of 24m and 32m cut into the longest possible equal lengths.

Concept: GCD(24, 32) = 8 m.

9. Traffic Lights: Changes every 45s and 60s. After how many minutes do they sync?

Concept: LCM(45, 60) = 180s = 3 minutes.

10. Tiling a Floor: A 120cm by 160cm area using the largest possible square tiles. Tile size?

Concept: GCD(120, 160) = 40 cm.
