1. 3D Coordinate Foundations (Midpoint, Centroid, Distance)

Focus: Calculating coordinates and distances in 3D space. (The plane is referred to an orthonormal system (O;$\vec{i}$,$\vec{j}$,$\vec{k}$))

10 Solved Examples

1. Find the midpoint of the segment joining A(1, 3, 5) and B(2, 4, 3).
Solution:
\(M(\frac{1+2}{2}, \frac{3+4}{2}, \frac{5+3}{2}) = (1.5, 3.5, 4)\)
2. Calculate the centroid of triangle PQR with P(1, 2, 3), Q(4, 5, 6), and R(7, 8, 9).
Solution:
\(G(\frac{1+4+7}{3}, \frac{2+5+8}{3}, \frac{3+6+9}{3}) = (4, 5, 6)\)
3. Find the distance between points X(1,0,0) and Y(4,4,0).
Solution:
\(\text{Distance} = \sqrt{(4-1)^2 + (4-0)^2 + (0-0)^2} = \sqrt{9+16} = 5\) units.
4. Point M(2, 2, 2) is the midpoint of [AB]. If A is (0, -1, 4), find B(x, y, z).
Solution:
\(2 = \frac{0+x}{2} \Rightarrow x=4\)
\(2 = \frac{-1+y}{2} \Rightarrow y=5\)
\(2 = \frac{4+z}{2} \Rightarrow z=0\).
B(4, 5, 0)
5. Find the length of the vector \(\vec{v} = (2, -1, 3)\).
Solution:
\(\lvert \vec{v} \rvert = \sqrt{2^2 + (-1)^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{4+1+9} = \sqrt{14}\)
6. Find the center of a sphere with diameter endpoints P(-1, 5, 2) and Q(3, -1, 4).
Solution:
Center is midpoint of $[PQ]$: \(M(\frac{-1+3}{2}, \frac{5-1}{2}, \frac{2+4}{2}) = (1, 2, 3)\)
7. Determine if the points A(1, 1, 1), B(2, 3, 4), and C(3, 5, 7) are collinear.
Solution:
$\overrightarrow{AB} = (1,2,3)$
$\overrightarrow{BC} = (1,2,3)$
So, $\overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{BC}$
Yes, A, B and C are collinear.
8. Find the coordinates of point D such that ABCD is a parallelogram, with A(0,0,0), B(1,0,0), C(1,1,0).
Solution:
$\overrightarrow{AD}=\overrightarrow{BC}$
$\overrightarrow{BC}=(0,1,0)$
So, $\overrightarrow{AD}=(0,1,0)$
Therefore, $D(0,1,0)$.
9. Determine the real numbers \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) so that A(2, 3, -1), B(\(\alpha, \beta, 3\)), and C(4, -2, 1) are collinear points.
Solution:
\(\overrightarrow{AC} = (2, -5, 2)\) and \(\overrightarrow{AB} = (\alpha-2, \beta-3, 4)\).
\(\overrightarrow{AB} = k\overrightarrow{AC} \Rightarrow 4 = 2k \Rightarrow k = 2\).
\(\alpha-2 = 2(2) \Rightarrow \alpha=6\) and \(\beta-3 = 2(-5) \Rightarrow \beta=-7\).
10. Show that A(1, 1, 0), B(2, 3, 1), C(0, 2, 2), and D(4, 4, 0) are coplanar.
Solution:
\(\overrightarrow{AB}=(1, 2, 1), \overrightarrow{AC}=(-1, 1, 2), \overrightarrow{AD}=(3, 3, 0)\).
\(\overrightarrow{AD} = \alpha\overrightarrow{AB} + \beta\overrightarrow{AC} \Rightarrow \alpha-\beta=3, 2\alpha+\beta=3 \Rightarrow \alpha=2, \beta=-1\).
Verification: \(1(2) + 2(-1) = 0\). Since \(0=0\), points are coplanar.

2. Vector Collinearity and Coplanarity

Focus: Conditions for vectors to be parallel, points on a line, and linear combinations (\(\vec{w}=x\vec{u}+y\vec{v}\)).

10 Solved Examples

1. Are vectors \(\vec{u} = (3, 4, 5)\) and \(\vec{v} = (6, 8, 10)\) collinear?
Solution:
\(\vec{v} = 2\vec{u}\). Since a scalar \(k=2\) exists, they are collinear.
2. Verify if A(1, 2, 3), B(2, 4, 6), and C(3, 6, 9) are collinear.
Solution:
$\overrightarrow{AB}(1, 2, 3) = \overrightarrow{BC}(1, 2, 3)$.
They are parallel and share a point, so collinear.
3. Express \(\vec{w} = (2, 5, 4)\) as a linear combination of \(\vec{u} = (1, 2, 1)\) and \(\vec{v} = (0, 1, 2)\).
Solution:
\(\vec{w} = 2\vec{u} + \vec{v}\).
4. Find point D such that $\overrightarrow{AD}$ = 2$\overrightarrow{AB}$ + 3$\overrightarrow{AC}$ given A(1, 0, 1), B(2, 1, 3), and C(0, 5, 2).
Solution:
D = (0, 17, 8).
5. Check if points P(1,0,0), Q(0,1,0), R(0,0,1), and S(1,1,1) are coplanar.
Solution:
Check if $\overrightarrow{PQ}, \overrightarrow{PR}, \overrightarrow{PS}$ are coplanar.
$\overrightarrow{PQ}=(-1,1,0), \overrightarrow{PR}=(-1,0,1), \overrightarrow{PS}=(0,1,1)$.
They are not coplanar.
6. Find the value of \(k\) for which vectors \(\vec{a}=(1, 2, 3)\) and \(\vec{b}=(k, 4, 6)\) are collinear.
Solution:
Ratios must be equal: \(\frac{k}{1} = \frac{4}{2} = \frac{6}{3} \Rightarrow k=2\).
7. Given \(\vec{u}=(1,0,0)\), \(\vec{v}=(0,1,0)\), \(\vec{w}=(0,0,1)\), are they coplanar?
Solution:
No, they are the standard basis vectors defining 3D space.
8. Determine if point M(5, 7, 9) lies on the line passing through A(1, 1, 1) and B(3, 4, 5).
Solution:
$\overrightarrow{AB}=(2, 3, 4)$ and $\overrightarrow{AM}=(4, 6, 8)$. Since $\overrightarrow{AM} = 2\overrightarrow{AB}$, the point M is collinear with A and B.
9. Find the coordinates of point G such that $\overrightarrow{OG} = \vec{u} + \vec{v} + \vec{w}$ where $\vec{u}=(2, -1, 4)$, $\vec{v}=(1, 5, -2)$, and $\vec{w}=(-3, 2, 1)$.
Solution:
G(2+1-3, -1+5+2, 4-2+1) = (0, 6, 3).
10. Are the vectors $\vec{a}=(1, 2, 1)$, $\vec{b}=(2, 3, 0)$, and $\vec{c}=(3, 5, 1)$ coplanar?
Solution:
Check if $\vec{c} = \vec{a} + \vec{b}$. Since $(1+2, 2+3, 1+0) = (3, 5, 1)$, $\vec{c}$ is a linear combination of $\vec{a}$ and $\vec{b}$. They are coplanar.

3. Vectors in 3D Figures (Coordinate Geometry Applications)

Focus: Establishing coordinate systems on figures (cubes, parallelepipeds, tetrahedrons) and using coordinates to prove geometric properties.

5 Solved Examples with Coordinates

1. Intersection of Diagonals in a Parallelepiped

In a parallelepiped \(OABCDEFG\) of edges \(OA = 4, OD = 3, OC = 2\), a coordinate system is defined by \((O;\frac{1}{4}\overrightarrow{OA},\frac{1}{2}\overrightarrow{OC},\frac{1}{3}\overrightarrow{OD})\). Prove that the main diagonals \([OF]\) and \([AG]\) bisect each other at a point P.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Determine coordinates of all necessary points.

\(O(0,0,0)\), \(A(4,0,0)\), \(C(0,2,0)\), \(B(4,2,0)\), \(G(0,2,3)\), \(F(4,2,3)\).

**Step 2:** Find the midpoint P of diagonal \([OF]\) using the midpoint formula:

\(P\left(\frac{0+4}{2}, \frac{0+2}{2}, \frac{0+3}{2}\right) = (2, 1, 1.5)\)

**Step 3:** Find the midpoint Q of diagonal \([AG]\) using the midpoint formula:

\(Q\left(\frac{4+0}{2}, \frac{0+2}{2}, \frac{0+3}{2}\right) = (2, 1, 1.5)\)

**Answer:** The main diagonals \([OF]\) and \([AG]\) have the same midpoint.

2. Collinearity in a Tetrahedron using Coordinates

\(ABCD\) is a tetrahedron, \(F\) is the midpoint of \([CD]\). Space is referred to the system \((A; \overrightarrow{AB}, \overrightarrow{AC}, \overrightarrow{AD})\).

Show that G, H and I are collinear on a line parallel to \((CD)\).

**Solution:**

The coordinates of the vertices in this system are \(A(0,0,0)\), \(B(1,0,0)\), \(C(0,1,0)\), and \(D(0,0,1)\).

G, center of gravity of triangle \(ABC\):

$$x_G = \frac{x_A + x_B + x_C}{3} = \frac{0+1+0}{3} = \frac{1}{3}$$ $$y_G = \frac{y_A + y_B + y_C}{3} = \frac{0+0+1}{3} = \frac{1}{3}$$ $$z_G = \frac{z_A + z_B + z_C}{3} = \frac{0+0+0}{3} = 0$$ $$G(\frac{1}{3}, \frac{1}{3}, 0)$$

H, center of gravity of triangle \(ABD\):

$$H(\frac{1}{3}, 0, \frac{1}{3})$$

I, center of gravity of triangle \(ABF\):

$$I(\frac{1}{3}, \frac{1}{6}, \frac{1}{6})$$

Coordinates of the vectors \(\overrightarrow{GH}\) and \(\overrightarrow{GI}\):

$$\overrightarrow{GH}(0,-\frac{1}{3}, \frac{1}{3})$$ $$\overrightarrow{GI}(0,-\frac{1}{6}, \frac{1}{6})$$

Relation between the two vectors:

$$\overrightarrow{GH} = 2 \thinspace \overrightarrow{GI}$$

Therefore, G, H and I are collinear.

Coordinates of the vector \(\overrightarrow{CD}\):

$$\overrightarrow{CD}(0,-1, 1)$$

Relation between the vectors:

$$\overrightarrow{CD} = 3 \thinspace \overrightarrow{GH}$$

Therefore, the lines \((CD)\) and \((GH)\) are parallel.

3. Finding a Missing Coordinate using Coordinates

**Problem: Points A(1, 0, 2), B(m, 3, 1), and C(5, 6, 0) are collinear. Find the value of m using coordinate properties.

**Solution:**

Step 1: If points are collinear, the components of vectors between them must be proportional.

\(\vec{AB} = (m-1, 3, -1)\)

\(\vec{BC} = (5-m, 3, -1)\)

Step 2: The ratio of components must be equal for parallel vectors (k is the scalar multiple):

\(\frac{3}{3} = \frac{-1}{-1} = 1 \implies k=1\)

Step 3: Apply the ratio to the first components:

\(\frac{m-1}{5-m} = 1 \implies m-1 = 5-m \implies 2m = 6 \implies m=3\)

**Answer:** The value of m is 3.

4. Proving a Figure is a Rhombus in 3D using Coordinates

Problem: Vertices \(A(0, 0, 0), B(2, 2, 1), C(3, 4, 3), \) and \(D(1, 2, 2)\) form a quadrilateral in an orthonormal system $(O;\vec{i},\vec{j},\vec{k})$. Show that the quadrilateral \(ABCD\) is a rhombus.

**Solution:**

$$ AB = \sqrt{2^2 + 2^2 + 1^2} = \sqrt{4 + 4 + 1} = 3 $$ $$ BC = \sqrt{1^2 + 2^2 + 2^2} = 3 $$ $$ CD = \sqrt{(-2)^2 + (-2)^2 + (-1)^2} = 3 $$ $$ DA = \sqrt{(-1)^2 + (-2)^2 + (-2)^2} = 3 $$

Step 2: All side lengths are equal to 3 units.

Answer: Since all four side lengths are equal, the quadrilateral \(ABCD\) is a rhombus.

5. Collinearity of P, Q, and R in a Cube

**Problem:** In a cube with side length 1, the space is referred to the system \((A; \overrightarrow{AB}, \overrightarrow{AC}, \overrightarrow{AD})\). Prove that the points $P$ (center of the face $ABFE$), $Q$ (center of the face $DCGH$), and $R$ (center of the cube) are collinear on a line parallel to the edge $(BC)$.

**Solution:**

**Step 1:** Determine coordinates of all necessary points.

\(A(0,0,0)\), \(B(1,0,0)\), \(C(1,1,0)\), \(D(0,1,0)\), \(E(0,0,1)\), \(F(1,0,1)\), \(G(1,1,1)\), \(H(0,1,1)\).

**Step 2:** Find the center P of the square \(ABFE\):

\(P\left(\frac{0+1}{2}, \frac{0+0}{2}, \frac{0+1}{2}\right) = (\frac{1}{2}, 0, \frac{1}{2})\)

**Step 2:** Find the center Q of the square \(DCGH\):

\(Q\left(\frac{1+0}{2}, \frac{1+1}{2}, \frac{0+1}{2}\right) = (\frac{1}{2}, 1, \frac{1}{2})\)

**Step 3:** Find the center R of the cube:

\(R\left(\frac{0+1}{2}, \frac{0+1}{2}, \frac{0+1}{2}\right) = (\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2})\)

Coordinates of the vectors \(\overrightarrow{PR}\) and \(\overrightarrow{PQ}\):

$$\overrightarrow{PR}(0,\frac{1}{2}, 0)$$ $$\overrightarrow{PQ}(0,1, 0)$$

Relation between the two vectors:

$$\overrightarrow{PQ} = 2 \thinspace \overrightarrow{PR}$$

Therefore, P, Q and R are collinear.

Coordinates of the vector \(\overrightarrow{BC}\):

$$\overrightarrow{BC}(0,1, 0)$$

Relation between the vectors:

$$\overrightarrow{BC} = \thinspace \overrightarrow{PQ}$$

Therefore, the lines \((BC)\) and \((PQ)\) are parallel.
