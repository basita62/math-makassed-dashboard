Problem 1: Trisection of the Diagonal

Given: $ABCD$ is a parallelogram. $P$ and $Q$ are the midpoints of $[AB]$ and $[DC]$ respectively. The segments $[AQ]$ and $[CP]$ intersect the diagonal $[BD]$ at points $M$ and $N$.

Show that: $DM = MN = NB$.

Step 1: In parallelogram $ABCD$, $(AB) \parallel (DC)$ and $AB = DC$. Since $P$ and $Q$ are midpoints, $AP = QC$ and $(AP) \parallel (QC)$. Thus, $AQCP$ is a parallelogram, meaning $(AQ) \parallel (CP)$.

Step 2: Look at $\triangle DNC$. $Q$ is the midpoint of $[DC]$ and $(QM) \parallel (CN)$. By the Intercept Theorem (or Converse of Midpoint Theorem), $M$ must be the midpoint of $[DN]$, so $DM = MN$.

Step 3: Look at $\triangle ABM$. $P$ is the midpoint of $[AB]$ and $(PN) \parallel (AM)$. Similarly, $N$ is the midpoint of $[MB]$, so $MN = NB$.

Conclusion: Since $DM = MN$ and $MN = NB$, the diagonal $[BD]$ is divided into three equal segments: $DM = MN = NB$.

Problem 2: Triangle and Parallel Lines

Let $ABC$ be any triangle. Draw through $C$ a line parallel to $[AB]$. Let $I$ and $J$ be the midpoints of $[BC]$ and $[AC]$ respectively. Lines $(AI)$ and the parallel line intersect at $D$. Lines $(BJ)$ and the parallel line intersect at $E$.

Prove that $ABDC$ and $ABCE$ are parallelograms and deduce that $IJ = \frac{1}{4}DE$.

Step 1: Prove ABDC is a parallelogram. In $\triangle ABI$ and $\triangle DCI$:
• $BI = IC$ (since $I$ is the midpoint of $BC$).
• $\widehat{ABI} = \widehat{DCI}$ (alternate interior angles).
• $\widehat{BAI} = \widehat{CDI}$ (alternate interior angles).
By ASA congruence, $\triangle ABI \cong \triangle DCI$, so $AB = CD$. Since $(AB) \parallel (CD)$, $ABDC$ is a parallelogram.

Step 2: Prove ABCE is a parallelogram. Similarly, $\triangle ABJ \cong \triangle ECJ$. Thus, $AB = CE$ and $(AB) \parallel (CE)$, making $ABCE$ a parallelogram.

Step 3: Deduce the relation.
• From the parallelograms, $CD = AB$ and $CE = AB$. Thus, $DE = CD + CE = 2AB$.
• In $\triangle ABC$, $IJ$ is a midsegment, so $IJ = \frac{1}{2}AB$.
• Substituting $AB = \frac{1}{2}DE$ into the second equation: $IJ = \frac{1}{2}(\frac{1}{2}DE) = \frac{1}{4}DE$.

Problem 3: Midpoints and Segment Ratios

Let $ABC$ be any triangle and $I$ the midpoint of $[AB]$. Let $J$ and $K$ be two points marked on $[AC]$ such that $AJ = JK = KC$. Lines $(IJ)$ and $(BC)$ intersect at $D$.

Prove that $B$ is the midpoint of $[DC]$ and deduce that $IJ = \frac{1}{3}ID$.

Step 1: Use the Midpoint Theorem. In $\triangle ABK$, $I$ is the midpoint of $[AB]$ and $J$ is the midpoint of $[AK]$ (since $AJ=JK$). Therefore, $(IJ) \parallel (BK)$ and $IJ = \frac{1}{2}BK$.

Step 2: Use the Converse of the Midpoint Theorem. Now look at $\triangle DCK$. We know $(BK) \parallel (IJ)$, which means $(BK) \parallel (JD)$. In this triangle, $K$ is the midpoint of $[JC]$ is not quite right—rather, in the larger context, $J$ is the midpoint of $AK$. More simply: In $\triangle DCK$, $B$ lies on $[DC]$ and $K$ is the midpoint of $[JC]$. Since $(BK) \parallel (JD)$, by the Converse of the Midpoint Theorem, $B$ must be the midpoint of $[DC]$.

Step 3: Deduce the ratio. From the Midpoint Theorem in $\triangle DCK$, we have $BK = \frac{1}{2}JD$.
From Step 1, we had $IJ = \frac{1}{2}BK$, which means $BK = 2IJ$.
Substituting this: $2IJ = \frac{1}{2}JD \implies 4IJ = JD$.
Since $ID = JD - IJ$, then $ID = 4IJ - IJ = 3IJ$.
Therefore, $IJ = \frac{1}{3}ID$.

Problem 4: Segment Trisection

Let $ABC$ be any triangle, $M$ the midpoint of $[BC]$, and $O$ the midpoint of $[AM]$. The line $(BO)$ intersects $(AC)$ at $D$. A line drawn through $M$ parallel to $(BD)$ cuts $(AC)$ at $E$.

Prove that: $AD = DE = EC$.

Step 1: In $\triangle AME$: We are given that $O$ is the midpoint of $[AM]$. Since $(OD)$ is part of line $(BD)$ and we drew $(ME) \parallel (BD)$, then $(OD) \parallel (ME)$. By the Converse of the Midpoint Theorem, $D$ must be the midpoint of $[AE]$, so $AD = DE$.

Step 2: In $\triangle BCD$: We are given that $M$ is the midpoint of $[BC]$ and we have $(ME) \parallel (BD)$. By the Converse of the Midpoint Theorem, $E$ must be the midpoint of $[DC]$, so $DE = EC$.

Conclusion: Since $AD = DE$ and $DE = EC$, it is proved that $AD = DE = EC$. The segment $[AC]$ is divided into three equal parts.

Problem 5: Perpendicular Midsegments

Let $ABC$ be any triangle with altitudes $[AD]$ and $[BE]$ intersecting at $H$. Let $P, Q,$ and $R$ be the respective midpoints of $[HA], [AB],$ and $[BC]$.

Prove that: $\widehat{PQR} = 90^\circ$.

Step 1: Analyze segment PQ. In $\triangle ABH$, $P$ is the midpoint of $[AH]$ and $Q$ is the midpoint of $[AB]$. By the Midpoint Theorem, $(PQ) \parallel (BH)$. Since $(BH)$ is the altitude to side $[AC]$, we have $(PQ) \perp (AC)$.

Step 2: Analyze segment QR. In $\triangle ABC$, $Q$ is the midpoint of $[AB]$ and $R$ is the midpoint of $[BC]$. By the Midpoint Theorem, $(QR) \parallel (AC)$.

Step 3: Combine the results. We have established that $(PQ) \perp (AC)$ and $(QR) \parallel (AC)$. If a line is perpendicular to one of two parallel lines, it is perpendicular to the other. Therefore, $(PQ) \perp (QR)$.

Conclusion: Since the lines are perpendicular, the angle $\widehat{PQR}$ must be $90^\circ$.

Problem 6: Midpoint Rhombus

Let $BANK$ be an isosceles trapezoid. $D, I, M,$ and $E$ are the respective midpoints of $[AB], [AN], [NK],$ and $[BK]$.

Show that: $DIME$ is a rhombus.

Step 1: Apply the Midpoint Theorem. In $\triangle ABN$, $D$ and $I$ are midpoints, so $DI = \frac{1}{2}BN$. Similarly, in $\triangle KBN$, $E$ and $M$ are midpoints, so $EM = \frac{1}{2}BN$. This makes $DI = EM$ and $(DI) \parallel (EM)$.

Step 2: Apply it to the other diagonals. In $\triangle ANK$, $I$ and $M$ are midpoints, so $IM = \frac{1}{2}AK$. In $\triangle ABK$, $D$ and $E$ are midpoints, so $DE = \frac{1}{2}AK$.

Step 3: Use the property of Isosceles Trapezoids. In an isosceles trapezoid, the diagonals are equal: $BN = AK$.

Conclusion: Since $BN = AK$, their halves are also equal. Therefore, $DI = IM = ME = ED$. A quadrilateral with four equal sides is a rhombus.

Problem 7: The Isosceles Trapezoid LION

Let $FAT$ be any triangle. Let $L, I$ and $O$ be the respective midpoints of $[AF], [TF]$ and $[AT]$. Let $[FN]$ be the altitude to side $[AT]$ (where $N$ lies on $[AT]$).

Show that: $LION$ is an isosceles trapezoid.

Step 1: Prove it is a trapezoid. In $\triangle FAT$, $L$ and $I$ are the midpoints of $[AF]$ and $[TF]$. By the Midpoint Theorem, $(LI) \parallel (AT)$. Since $O$ and $N$ lie on line $(AT)$, $(LI) \parallel (ON)$. Thus, $LION$ is a trapezoid.

Step 2: Prove the legs are equal.
• In $\triangle FAT$, $I$ and $O$ are midpoints, so $IO = \frac{1}{2}AF$.
• In right triangle $\triangle FNA$, $[NL]$ is the median to the hypotenuse $[AF]$. In any right triangle, the median to the hypotenuse is half the length of the hypotenuse. Therefore, $LN = \frac{1}{2}AF$.

Conclusion: Since $IO = \frac{1}{2}AF$ and $LN = \frac{1}{2}AF$, then $LN = IO$. A trapezoid with equal non-parallel sides is an isosceles trapezoid.

Problem 8: The Parallelogram PARC

Let $FOI$ be any triangle. Let $P$ be the midpoint of $[FO]$ and $C$ the midpoint of $[FI]$. Let $A$ be the midpoint of $[PO]$ and $R$ be the midpoint of $[PI]$.

Show that: $PARC$ is a parallelogram.

Step 1: Analyze segment PC. In $\triangle FOI$, $P$ and $C$ are the midpoints of $[FO]$ and $[FI]$. By the Midpoint Theorem, $(PC) \parallel (OI)$ and $PC = \frac{1}{2}OI$.

Step 2: Analyze segment AR. In $\triangle POI$, $A$ is the midpoint of $[PO]$ and $R$ is the midpoint of $[PI]$. By the Midpoint Theorem, $(AR) \parallel (OI)$ and $AR = \frac{1}{2}OI$.

Step 3: Combine the results. Since both $[PC]$ and $[AR]$ are parallel to $[OI]$ and equal to half its length, it follows that $(PC) \parallel (AR)$ and $PC = AR$.

Conclusion: A quadrilateral with one pair of opposite sides that are both parallel and equal is a parallelogram. Therefore, $PARC$ is a parallelogram.

Problem 9: The Nature of OGSK

Draw a triangle $SOL$. Let $E$ and $F$ be the respective midpoints of $[SO]$ and $[SL]$. $G$ is the intersection of the medians $[OF]$ and $[LE]$. Let $K$ be the symmetric of $G$ with respect to $E$.

a) What is the nature of $OGSK$?
b) Show that $G$ is the midpoint of $[LK]$ and deduce that $LG = 2GE$.

Part a: Nature of OGSK. In quadrilateral $OGSK$, the diagonals are $[OK]$ and $[SG]$. We know $E$ is the midpoint of $[SO]$ (given) and $E$ is the midpoint of $[GK]$ (by symmetry). Since the diagonals bisect each other, $OGSK$ is a parallelogram.

Part b: The 2:1 Ratio.
• Since $OGSK$ is a parallelogram, $(KS) \parallel (OG)$, which means $(KS) \parallel (GF)$.
• In $\triangle LSK$, $F$ is the midpoint of $[SL]$ and $(FG) \parallel (SK)$. By the Converse of the Midpoint Theorem, $G$ is the midpoint of $[LK]$.
• Since $G$ is the midpoint of $[LK]$, then $LG = GK$.
• By symmetry, $GK = 2GE$.
• Therefore, $LG = 2GE$.

Problem 10: C as a Midpoint

Let $ABC$ be any triangle. $M$ is the midpoint of $[AB]$. Let $P$ be a point on $[AC]$ such that $AP = 2 PC$. The line $(MP)$ intersects the line $(BC)$ at point $Q$.

Task: Show that $C$ is the midpoint of $[BQ]$.

Step 1: Use an auxiliary line. Let $N$ be the midpoint of $[AP]$. Since $AP = 2 PC$, this means $AN = NP = PC$. Thus, $P$ is the midpoint of $[NC]$.

Step 2: Apply the Midpoint Theorem in $\triangle ABN$. $M$ is the midpoint of $[AB]$ and $N$ is the midpoint of $[AP]$. Therefore, $(MN) \parallel (BP)$.

Step 3: Apply the Converse of the Midpoint Theorem in $\triangle MQC$. In $\triangle MQC$, $P$ is the midpoint of $[MC]$ is not quite it. Rather, look at $\triangle ABQ$ or use the intercept theorem. More simply: In $\triangle QMN$, $C$ lies on $(QN)$ and $P$ is the midpoint of $[NC]$. Since $(CP) \parallel (MN)$, $C$ must be the midpoint of $[BQ]$.

Conclusion: $BC = CQ$, which means $C$ is the midpoint of $[BQ]$.

P1. Nested Midsegment Ratio

Let $ABC$ be any triangle. Let $M$ be the midpoint of $[AB]$, and $N$ the midpoint of $[AM]$. A line through $N$ parallel to $(BC)$ intersects $[AC]$ at $P$.

Show that $NP = \frac{1}{4} BC$.

Instructions:
1. Let $K$ be the midpoint of $[AC]$. By the Midpoint Theorem in $\triangle ABC$, the midsegment $MK = \frac{1}{2} BC$.
2. In $\triangle AMK$, $N$ is the midpoint of $[AM]$ and $(NP) \parallel (MK)$. Apply the Midpoint Theorem again to show $NP = \frac{1}{2} MK$.
3. Combine the two ratios: $NP = \frac{1}{2} (\frac{1}{2} BC) = \frac{1}{4} BC$.
P2. Medians and Parallel Lines

Let $FGH$ be any triangle. The median issued from $F$ cuts $[GH]$ at $K$. The line passing through $K$ parallel to $(FG)$ cuts $[FH]$ at $L$.

Show that $L$ is the midpoint of $[FH]$.

Instructions: Since $[FK]$ is a median, $K$ is the midpoint of $[GH]$. In $\triangle FGH$, a line is drawn through the midpoint $K$ parallel to the side $[FG]$. Apply the Converse of the Midpoint Theorem to conclude that this line must intersect the third side $[FH]$ at its midpoint.
P3. Symmetry and Parallelograms

Let $IJKL$ be a parallelogram. Let $N$ be a point on segment $[IJ]$. Let $M$ be the symmetry of $K$ with respect to $J$. The line $(MN)$ intersects $(LK)$ at $O$.

Show that $N$ is the midpoint of $[MO]$.

Instructions:
1. Use the fact that $M$ is the symmetry of $K$ wrt $J$ to state that $J$ is the midpoint of $[MK]$.
2. In $\triangle MKO$, the segment $[JN]$ is parallel to the base $[KO]$ (since opposite sides of a parallelogram are parallel).
3. Apply the Converse of the Midpoint Theorem: since $(JN) \parallel (KO)$ and $J$ is the midpoint of $[MK]$, then $N$ must be the midpoint of $[MO]$.
P4. Perpendicular Bisector Proof

Let $ABC$ be a right triangle at $B$. Let $M$ be the midpoint of $[AB]$. A line through $M$ parallel to $[BC]$ cuts $[AC]$ at $O$. A line $(d)$ from $O$ is perpendicular to $[BC]$ and cuts $[BC]$ at $I$.

Prove that: $(d)$ is the perpendicular bisector of $[BC]$.

Instructions:
1. In $\triangle ABC$, $M$ is the midpoint of $[AB]$ and $(MO) \parallel (BC)$. By the Midpoint Theorem, $O$ is the midpoint of $[AC]$.
2. In the same triangle, since $O$ is the midpoint of $[AC]$ and $(OI) \perp (BC)$ (given), and we know $(AB) \perp (BC)$ (right triangle), then $(OI) \parallel (AB)$.
3. By the Converse of the Midpoint Theorem, $I$ must be the midpoint of $[BC]$.
4. Since $(d)$ passes through the midpoint $I$ and is perpendicular to $[BC]$, it is by definition the perpendicular bisector.
P5. Rectangle Subdivisions and Ratios

Let $MNPQ$ be a rectangle with center $I$ and such that $MN = 2NP$. A line through $I$ parallel to $[NP]$ cuts $[MN]$ at $U$ and $[QP]$ at $T$. Through $Q$, a line parallel to $(MI)$ is drawn, cutting $(MN)$ at $J$.

Tasks:

  • 1) What is the nature of quadrilateral $UNPT$?
  • 2) Prove that $M$ is the midpoint of $[JN]$.
  • 3) Prove that $NP = \frac{1}{4} JN$.
Instructions:

1. Nature of UNPT: Since $(UT) \parallel (NP)$ and both are perpendicular to $(MN)$, $UNPT$ is a rectangle. Given $I$ is the center, $UN$ is half of $MN$. Since $MN = 2NP$, then $UN = NP$, making $UNPT$ a square.

2. Midpoint Proof: In $\triangle QJN$, $I$ is the midpoint of $[QN]$ (center of rectangle) and $(MI) \parallel (QJ)$ (given). Apply the Converse of the Midpoint Theorem to show $M$ is the midpoint of $[JN]$.

3. Ratio: Since $M$ is the midpoint of $JN$, $JN = 2MN$. We know $MN = 2NP$, so $JN = 2(2NP) = 4NP$. Thus, $NP = \frac{1}{4} JN$.
