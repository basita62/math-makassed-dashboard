1. Equations involving $^nP_r$ and $^nC_r$

Solving for unknown variables using factorial definitions.

5 Solved Exercises

1. Solve $^nP_2 = 110$
$n(n-1) = 110 \Rightarrow n^2 - n - 110 = 0$
$(n-11)(n+10) = 0 \Rightarrow \mathbf{n=11}$
2. Solve $^nC_{n-2} = 15$
$^nC_{n-2} = ^nC_2 = \frac{n(n-1)}{2} = 15$
$n(n-1) = 30 \Rightarrow \mathbf{n=6}$
3. Solve $^nP_4 = 20 \times ^nP_2$
$n(n-1)(n-2)(n-3) = 20n(n-1)$
$(n-2)(n-3) = 20 \Rightarrow n^2-5n-14=0 \Rightarrow \mathbf{n=7}$
4. If $^nC_8 = ^nC_{12}$, find $n$
Rule: $^nC_x = ^nC_y \Rightarrow x+y=n$
$8+12 = 20 \Rightarrow \mathbf{n=20}$
5. Solve $2 \cdot ^nC_2 = ^nP_2 - 0$
$2 \frac{n(n-1)}{2} = n(n-1) \Rightarrow$ True for all $n \ge 2$.

Applying Addition and Multiplication principles to real-world scenarios.

9. 2 dice rolled. Total outcomes? $6 \times 6 = \mathbf{36}$

3. Counting Problems: Advanced Conditions

Problems involving specific constraints on seating, grouping, and arrangements.

10 Solved Exercises

1. 5-digit even numbers from {1,2,3,4,5} (use once)
Ends in 2 or 4 (2 options). Remaining 4 digits arrange in $4!$ ways.
$2 \times 4! = 2 \times 24 = \mathbf{48}$ ways.
2. Man and 2 women in 5 chairs; women together
Treat 2 women as 1 block. Arrange M, W-W, Empty, Empty in $4!$ ways.
Women can swap within block (2! ways). $4! \times 2! = 24 \times 2 = \mathbf{48}$ ways.
3. Team of 11: exactly 1 Math teacher from 2 available
Choose 1 Math ($^2C_1$), choose 10 others from 14 non-math teachers ($^{14}C_{10}$).
$^2C_1 \times ^{14}C_{10} = 2 \times 1001 = \mathbf{2002}$ ways.
4. 7-char password from 12 chars (no repeat), starts with digit
Choose 1st digit (4 options). Choose and arrange 6 remaining from 11 ($^{11}P_6$).
$4 \times \frac{11!}{5!} = 4 \times 332640 = \mathbf{1,330,560}$ ways.
5. Arrange 11 TVs: no two Company A sets (6 total) next to each other
Arrange 5 B sets first: $5!$ ways. Creates 6 spaces ($\_B\_B\_B\_B\_B\_$).
Place 6 A sets into these 6 spaces: $^6P_6 = 6!$ ways.
$5! \times 6! = 120 \times 720 = \mathbf{86,400}$ ways.
6. Group of 4 people: exactly 3 women from 5W, 3M
Choose 3 women from 5 ($^5C_3$) AND 1 man from 3 ($^3C_1$).
$^5C_3 \times ^3C_1 = 10 \times 3 = \mathbf{30}$ ways.
7. Select 2 questions from A(5), 4 from B(8)
$^5C_2 \times ^8C_4 = 10 \times 70 = \mathbf{700}$ ways.
8. Team of 6 (4M, 2F): Mr & Mrs Coldicott must both be in team
Mr C is 1 of the 4 males (need 3 more from remaining 4 sons). Mrs C is 1 of 2 females (need 1 more from remaining 3 daughters).
$^4C_3 \times ^3C_1 = 4 \times 3 = \mathbf{12}$ ways.
9. 5-digit code from {1..9}, first digit 6 and rest odd
1st digit fixed as 6 (1 option). Need 4 odd digits from {1,3,5,7,9} (5 options), arranged.
$1 \times ^5P_4 = 1 \times 120 = \mathbf{120}$ ways.
10. Team of 3: more women than men (7W, 6M)
Options: (3W, 0M) OR (2W, 1M).
$^7C_3 \times ^6C_0 + ^7C_2 \times ^6C_1 = (35 \times 1) + (21 \times 6) = 35 + 126 = \mathbf{161}$ ways.

4. Exam Problems

Problems sourced from GCE A-Level, IB, and AMC/AIME standardized exams.

10 Solved Exam-Style Examples

1. 15 boys, 10 girls sit in a single line in two separate groups (all boys then all girls, or vice versa). Ways?
Arrange boys ($15!$), arrange girls ($10!$). Two orders possible (BG or GB).
$2 \times 15! \times 10!$ ways.
2. Select a team of 4 males and 2 females from 5 sons and 4 daughters, with no restrictions. Ways?
Choose 4 males from 5 ($^5C_4$), choose 2 females from 4 ($^4C_2$).
$^5C_4 \times ^4C_2 = 5 \times 6 = \mathbf{30}$ ways.
3. 25 knights at a round table. Choose 3. Probability at least 2 are neighbors?
Total ways $C(25, 3) = 2300$. Ways with exactly 2 neighbors + Ways with 3 neighbors $= (25 \times 21) + 25 = 550$. Probability $\frac{550}{2300} = \mathbf{\frac{11}{46}}$.
4. Permutations of 4 letters from "SUNDAY" with at least one vowel (A or U)?
Total 4-letter permutations - Permutations with no vowels.
$^6P_4 - (^4P_4) = 360 - 24 = \mathbf{336}$ ways.
5. 5-digit number > 60,000 using {1,2,3,4,5,6,7,8} once. Ways?
First digit must be 6, 7, or 8 (3 options). Remaining 4 digits from remaining 7 options: $^7P_4$.
$3 \times 840 = \mathbf{2520}$ ways.
6. A 4-digit code using 1-9 once is even. Ways?
Ends in 2, 4, 6, or 8 (4 options). Remaining 3 digits from remaining 8 options: $^8P_3$.
$4 \times 336 = \mathbf{1344}$ ways.
7. A 5-char code from A-F (6) and 1-7 (7) has no two letters or two numbers follow each other (alternating L/N). Ways?
Must start with L or N. Starts LNLNL: $^6P_3 \times ^7P_2 = 1260$. Starts NLNLN: $^7P_3 \times ^6P_2 = 2520$.
Total: $1260 + 2520 = \mathbf{3780}$ ways.
8. Committee of 8 from 9 men and 5 women, with at least 4 women. Ways?
(4W, 4M) + (5W, 3M) = $(^5C_4 \times ^9C_4) + (^5C_5 \times ^9C_3)$
$= (5 \times 126) + (1 \times 84) = 630 + 84 = \mathbf{714}$ ways.
9. Select 4 cards from 52, exactly one Ace. Ways?
Choose 1 Ace from 4 ($^4C_1$), choose 3 non-Aces from 48 ($^{48}C_3$).
$4 \times 17296 = \mathbf{69,184}$ ways.
10. Team of 6 (4M, 2F): either Mr *or* Mrs Coldicott is in the team, but not both. Ways?
(Mr included, Mrs excluded) + (Mrs included, Mr excluded).
$(^4C_3 \times ^3C_2) + (^4C_4 \times ^3C_1) = (4 \times 3) + (1 \times 3) = 12 + 3 = \mathbf{15}$ ways.
